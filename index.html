Uncertainty in the Inferred Metallicity of Gas Giant Exoplanets

Maya Ben-Yami, Simon Müller, Ravit Helled

Motivation

The bulk composition of an exoplanet not only characterises the planet but provides important constraints for planet formation theory.

For non-inflated giant exoplanets the bulk heavy element content can be inferred from mass-radius measurements with theoretical thermal evolution models (Thorngren et al. 2016).

As observational accuracy improves it is important to develop theoretical models.

Aims

We investigate how model assumptions affect the inferred bulk composition of giant exoplanets, and compare theoretical to observational uncertainty.

In particular, we consider:

  • The H-He Equation of State (EoS)
  • The distribution of heavy elements: core + envelope vs homogeneously mixed structure
  • The effect of heavy elements on the opacity

Methods

We calculated the long-term thermal evolution of gaseous planets with the Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics code (Paxton et al. 2019), release 10108 with a modified EoS (Müller et al. 2020).

For a planet of a given mass and bulk composition the theoretical calculation solves the 1D structure and evolution equations. We focus on the resulting radius evolution.

Conclusions

Theoretical uncertainties can be comparable to (or larger than) measurement uncertainties.
Therefore improvements in theory are required in order to interpret future data.

  • This introduces new inflated warm gas giants
  • It is important to account for the contribution of heavy elements to the opacity.

H-He Equation of State

Here, we investigate the influence of two different H-He EoSs on the planetary inferred radius. We compare the widely used SCvH (Saumon et al. 1995) EoS, as well as its updated version CMS (Chabrier et al. 2019) (see methods). For all the cases, we keep the H-He ratio at the protosolar value.

In the first figure we show the contraction of a 0.4 \(M_J\) planet for three different mass fractions of water (0, 0.2 and 0.3) homogeneously mixed within the interior. The synthetic planet receives a stellar flux of \(10^{7}\) erg cm\(^{-2}s^{-1}\), and we allow the gas opacity to scale with the metallicity of the envelope.

At a given age and metallicity, the CMS EoS yields a planet that is more compact than the one calculated with SCvH. This is because hydrogen is denser under certain pressures and temperatures in CMS compared to SCvH . As a result, the effect of using these two EoSs is more profound for lower \(Z\), where the hydrogen mass fraction is larger. Note that the zero metallicity line for CMS is very close to overlapping with the \(Z = 0.2\) line for SCvH. This shows that the change in radius caused by only changing the H-He EoS can induce a large change in the determination of the planet’s metallicity.

Next, for each of the EoSs, we calculated the thermal evolution of synthetic planets until the age of 3 Gyrs on a mass-metallicity grid. The masses range from \(0.2 - 3\) Jupiter masses and the planetary bulk metallicity is either \(Z = 0, 0.2\) or \(0.4\). The heavy-element distribution, the flux and opacity are the same as in the previous case. In the second figure we plot the calculated radius at 3 Gyrs vs. the planetary mass for SCvH (dashed lines) and CMS (solid lines). As a reference we also plot some of the planets with a similar age from the Thorngren et al. 2016 catalogue with their observational uncertainties. Again, it is clear that the difference in radius is more profound when the bulk metallicity is low. In addition, the effect is more significant for smaller masses. At masses \(< 1 M_J\), the difference can be as large as nearly 10 %, while beyond \(\simeq 2 M_J\) it is at most a few percent. The \(Z = 0.2\) lines calculated with CMS and \(Z = 0\) calculated with SCvH intersect at about \(0.5 M_J\). The change in radius is sufficient to significantly influence the inferred planetary metallicity. The key conclusion to be taken from this figure is that even if the mass, radius, and age of a giant planet are accurately determined, the inferred metallicity could vary by up to 20% of the planet's mass when using different H-He EoSs.

Effect of Metals on Opacity

The Freedman et al. 2014 opacity accounts for the heavy elements present in the gas (see methods). This enhanced opacity can delay the cooling of the planet, leading to a larger radius at a given age. We investigate its effect by comparing two distributions of the heavy elements: one where all the heavy elements are placed in a central core surrounded by a H-He envelope, and one where they are evenly mixed in the planetary interior. Only in the mixed case do the heavy elements affect the opacity. The distribution of heavy elements has been shown to affect the radius even in models that do not account for the opacity (Baraffe et al. 2008, Thorngren et al. 2016), but that effect is small compared to the change in radius caused by the metallicity-scaled opacity.

This figure shows the Radius vs. planetary bulk metallicity of a \(0.2 M_J\) and a \(1 M_J\) planet at 0.5 and 5 Gyrs. All the heavy elements are placed in a core (dashed lines) or homogeneously mixed (solid lines). The colors indicate the stellar irradiation received by the planet.

When all the heavy elements are placed in a core we see the behavior we would intuitively expect: the radius decreases as metallicity increases. This is also the case for the mixed models when they are older and/or less irradiated, because for low temperatures the gas opacity does not scale strongly with the metallicity. But for the mixed models with hot outer envelopes there is a non-monotone behavior in the Radius-Metallicity relation. There is a competition between the increase in density with larger \(Z\) and the delayed cooling due to the higher opacity. This causes the radius to initially increase with \(Z\), reach a maximum at \(Z \simeq 0.1\) and then decrease monotonically.

This means that the largest possible planet is not necessarily purely composed of H-He. It also introduces an additional degeneracy when inferring a planet's metallicity: even if age, radius, mass and the heavy-element distribution of a planet were perfectly known, there would still be an ambiguity in the inferred heavy-element mass if the opacity or the heavy-element distribution are not well-determined. For example, it can be seen in the Figure that for a 5 Gyrs old \(1 M_J\) planet receiving a stellar flux of \(10^{8}\) erg cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\), either \(Z = 0\) or \(Z \simeq 0.15\) could fit the same radius.

New Inflated Planets

We investigate the planets for which Thorngren et al. 2016 calculate a bulk metallicity using the CMS H-He EoS (Chabrier et al. 2019). CMS EoS gives smaller radii relative to the EoS Thorngren et al. use, SCvH (see section 1). Consequently for planets with a inferred bulk metallicity of \(Z \leq 0.1\) in the Thorngren et al. predictions our calculations with the new EoS predict a median value of \(Z = 0\) to fit the planet's radius. This was the case for 12 planets out of the 47 planets in their catalogue (see bottom figure).

For HAT-P-17b, HAT-P-18b, Kepler-89d, Kepler-117c, Kepler-1647b and WASP-69b even a pure H-He object with \(Z = 0.1\) is barely large enough to match their radius. The first figure below shows the observational values of these planets' radius and age together with the thermal evolution of a pure H-He planet with the same mass and irradiation. There seems to be no common factor to these planets: their mass, stellar irradiation, stellar metallicity and stellar mass cover a large range with no discernible pattern. If one assumes that the CMS EoS yields a more realistic planetary model, then these planets are expected to be inflated giant planets.

In the second figure we show the irradiation of both the inflated planets and those with a median value of \(Z = 0\). Inflated gas giants are generally taken to be hot Jupiters, receiving a large amount of irradiation from their star (Thorngren et al. 2016). Our new inflated planets are only warm, with insolation below \(10^8\) \(erg s^{−1} cm^{−2}\) .

Theoretical vs Observational Uncertainties

A crucial question is whether the differences between models can lead to theoretical uncertainties that are comparable (or even larger) than the observational ones.

The figure shows the radii and masses of 12 of the 47 exoplanets from Thorngren et al. 2016, together with the observational uncertainties in their radii (shaded grey rectangles). For each of these planets, we simulate the thermal evolution using the range of possible bulk metallicities as inferred in Thorngren et al. 2016. This is done for three model assumptions, with the coloured error bars representing the ranges of inferred radii at the most likely age for the planet:

  • CMS EoS with a fully mixed interior
  • CMS EoS with a core-envelope structure
  • SCvH EoS and a fully mixed interior

While the uncertainty within one given model (for example, comparing the orange error bars with grey rectangles) is by construction about the same as the observational uncertainty, the difference between the different models (colours) can be much larger. This is particularly true for planets like Kepler-89d or HATS-6b that have small observational error bars.

This clearly demonstrates that in many cases, the difference in radius between the different models is larger by a factor of a few than the observational uncertainties. In other words, even if observational techniques improve, from a modelling point of view a better estimate of the bulk metallicity could not be obtained. Therefore in order to interpret future observations properly progress in theory is required. This includes a better understanding of EoSs, opacities, as well as a linking of planet formation models to observations of the current-state properties.

Methods

In order to investigate the effect of model assumptions on the inferred metallicity of exoplanets, we simulate the thermal evolution of gas giants using the stellar evolution code Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA) (Paxton et al. 2019) with a modified EoS suitable for giant planets (Müller et al. 2020). We follow a similar approach as Thorngren et al. 2016: for a given mass, bulk metallicity, and stellar irradiation, MESA calculates how the planetary interior evolves with time. The planet's bulk metallicity is inferred by requiring that its radius at a particular age falls within the observational uncertainty. Thorngren et al. 2016 created a catalogue of non-highly irradiated exoplanets with known mass, radius and age measurements, and estimate the bulk metallicity of these planets. Here, we use this catalogue as well as synthetic planets and calculate their thermal evolution with different EoSs for H-He and opacities.

The modifications from Müller et al. 2020 to the MESA EoS make it applicable for planetary interiors with heavy-element mass fractions \(Z > 0\). This was achieved by calculating the thermodynamic variables for an ideal mixture of hydrogen, helium, and heavy elements, following Vazan et al. 2013. For H-He, both the SCvH (Saumon et al. 1995) and the EoS presented by Chabrier et al. 2019 (henceforth CMS) are implemented. For all the cases, we keep the H-He ratio at the protosolar value. The heavy elements are represented either by water or rock (SiO\(_2\)) using the QEoS (More et al. 1988, Vazan et al. 2013).
